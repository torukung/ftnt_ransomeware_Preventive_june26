01  Why this matters to the whole sector

One bank was named. Every bank is exposed.

LAPSUS$ has publicly claimed a full‑platform compromise of AYA Bank, including personally identifiable information and payment‑card data, with a threatened sale to a single buyer. The same intrusion pattern — identity‑led, low‑cost, repeatable — works against any institution that has not closed the same gaps. For the Central Bank of Myanmar, the exposure is systemic: a confidence shock to one bank can ripple across the payment system and public trust in digital banking.

02  Anatomy of the attack

An identity‑first kill chain — and where it breaks

LAPSUS$ is not known for zero‑day wizardry. Their playbook is human and identity‑centric: buy or phish credentials, defeat weak MFA, log in as a trusted user, move laterally, and exfiltrate for extortion. Each stage maps to a known MITRE ATT&CK technique — and each has a corresponding Fortinet control that stops it.

Two lessons for the sector: defend identity first (the attack begins and pivots on stolen logins), and segment aggressively — segmentation is the difference between “one compromised laptop” and “the entire core banking platform.”

04  The preventive blueprint, in phases

A firm, staged guideline — not a wish list

The same six control domains, sequenced into three enforceable phases. Phase 1 is the minimum baseline every licensed institution must meet; Phases 2–3 deepen resilience and scale with each bank’s tier — clear, measurable and supervisable.

PRINCIPLE 01

Identity first

Every login phishing‑resistant; every privilege controlled. The attack starts here — so does the defense.

PRINCIPLE 02

Segment always

No flat networks. Contain by default, so one foothold never becomes the whole platform.

PRINCIPLE 03

Assume breach

Detect fast, respond automatically, recover without paying. Resilience is the goal — not perfection.

08  A Central Bank of Myanmar–led rollout

From one bank’s breach to a sector’s baseline

The Central Bank of Myanmar is uniquely placed to convert this incident into sector‑wide resilience: issue a minimum‑controls baseline, supervise attestation, and stand up shared threat intelligence. A phased, tiered model keeps it achievable for banks of every size — from D‑SIBs to microfinance institutions.

Phase 1 · 0–90 days

Stop the AYA pattern

Close the identity gap that breaches begin with.

  • Phishing‑resistant MFA on all staff & admin access
  • Privileged‑access control for core banking
  • Email security + anti‑phishing on every mailbox
  • EDR deployed across all endpoints
  • Dark‑web / leaked‑credential monitoring live
Phase 2 · 3–9 months

Contain & see

Limit blast radius and gain full visibility.

  • Internal network segmentation around core systems
  • Zero‑trust access (ZTNA) replacing flat VPN
  • Centralized logging & SIEM correlation
  • Tested incident‑response playbooks
  • Mandatory incident reporting to CBM
Phase 3 · 9–18 months

Sector resilience

Move from per‑bank defense to shared defense.

  • CBM‑coordinated Financial‑sector CERT & threat sharing
  • Deception & advanced detection at Tier‑1 banks
  • Assured immutable backup & recovery drills
  • Continuous attestation & independent assessment
  • Sector cyber‑maturity dashboard for supervisors
Tier 1 — D‑SIBs & large banks

Full Security Fabric with 24×7 SOC, deception and threat‑hunting. Anchor nodes for sector threat sharing.

Tier 2 — mid‑size banks

Core Fabric (identity, email, EDR, segmentation, SIEM) with co‑managed SOC to offset lean teams.

Tier 3 — small banks & MFIs

SASE‑delivered baseline — identity, endpoint and segmentation as a managed service, low‑footprint.
